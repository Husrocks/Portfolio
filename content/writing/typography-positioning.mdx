There's a moment in every brand engagement when the typeface decision stops being about fonts and starts being about values.

I didn't understand this until I was three years into doing brand work full-time. Before that, I thought of type selection as a craft problem — you had criteria (legibility, personality, file size), you evaluated options against those criteria, you made a choice. Clean, rational.

Then I spent six months working with a fintech company that wanted to feel "approachable." We went through 14 type systems before I realized that the team's disagreements about typefaces weren't aesthetic disagreements — they were disagreements about what "approachable" actually meant, and who they were trying to be approachable *to*.

## Type as Positioning

A typeface makes a claim. It says: this is the kind of thing we are, this is the register we're speaking in, this is who we think you are.

When a healthcare startup chooses a humanist sans-serif with rounded terminals, they're not just picking something that tests well with users. They're saying: *we are warm, we are trustworthy, we don't want to feel like a hospital.* The type is doing positioning work.

This is why working from a brief is almost always the wrong approach. "We want something modern but classic, approachable but authoritative" doesn't describe a typeface — it describes an unresolved tension in the brand strategy. The type decision is the downstream effect of resolving that tension, not a way to avoid resolving it.

## The Test I Always Run

For the past four years, I've run every type system through a single question before I present it to a client:

*If this company published a physical newspaper, what would it look like?*

It's a strange question, but it works. Newspapers have solved every typographic problem a brand will ever face — hierarchy, legibility at scale, the relationship between display and text type, how to handle dense information environments. And because everyone has a felt sense of what different newspapers feel like (tabloid vs. broadsheet, local vs. national), the question surfaces assumptions quickly.

The answer isn't about literally using newspaper-style type. It's about what the answer reveals about the brand's relationship with authority, accessibility, and tone.

## What This Changed for Me

Once I started thinking about type as positioning, I stopped treating font decisions as something I could make and then defend. Instead, I started making them in front of clients — explaining the claim each option makes, and letting the choice emerge from the conversation.

It's slower. It requires clients who trust the process enough to engage with it. But the systems that come out of it are almost always more coherent, and they almost always survive longer than the ones I made for people.

That matters. A brand system isn't finished when it ships — it's finished when the company doesn't need you anymore to maintain it.
